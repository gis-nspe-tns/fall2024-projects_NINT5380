Tak Watanabe | Does Global Warming Affect Snowfall in Japan?

Snowfall patterns in Japan are undergoing significant changes, potentially due to global warming. Over recent decades, snowfall has diminished, posing a threat to lifestyles and economies reliant on snowy winters. This project analyzes data from 1969–1973 and 2019–2023, comparing temperatures and snowfall to gain insight into the unfolding changes.

Neven Armanios | Bangladesh - An analysis of high births rates against low lying flood risk areas

Bangladesh's terrain is generally flat and lies less than 10 meters above sea level. It is also one of the wettest countries in the world, with monsoon rains and frequent floods from June to September.

Despite all demographics in these areas being vulnerable, pregnant women are identified as the most vulnerable human beings during floods in Bangladesh. Maternal deaths are common in this period with several complications. I conducted a vulnerability analysis to identify the low lying areas that spatially coincide with locations in which high birth rates are prevalent across Bangladesh.

Amy Breedlove | Brooklyn Marine Terminal and the communities nearby, traffic and pollution what does the future hold?

The Brooklyn Marine Terminal site is a 122-acre site on New York City's waterfront, a project the City of New York and the Economic Development Corporation (EDC) plan to develop with modernized maritime uses, residential and commercial spaces as well as open public spaces and parks. Current operations on the site provide 500 jobs and actively cover only approximately 36% of the site. Development on the site will increase jobs through a modern maritime port, housing in new residential developments, retail and commercial spaces, a hotel, recreation space, public open space, and parks. What are the current traffic conditions and how might this new 122-acre development increase the negative impacts of traffic and congestion?

Hyeonjeong Kwon | Mapping Transit Gaps in High-Density Areas of New York City

This project analyzes the relationship between population density and public transit accessibility in New York City to identify underserved areas. Using spatial data, the study reclassified population density into categories (low, medium, high) and applied buffer zones of 500 feet for bus stops and 1,500 feet for subway stations. Areas lacking transit coverage were highlighted and intersected with population density data to pinpoint high-density zones with limited transit access. The analysis utilized geoprocessing methods such as reclassification, buffering, zonal statistics, and clipping to produce actionable insights. The results visualize critical gaps in transit accessibility, focusing on areas like Northern Manhattan and parts of Staten Island, where public transportation coverage remains insufficient despite significant population density. This research provides a foundation for urban planners and policymakers to prioritize transit investments, aiming to enhance equity and mobility in New York City’s most underserved neighborhoods.

Constanza Bucarey Andaur | Mapping Femicide Rates Across Latin America: Identifying Spatial Patterns and Critical Areas for Intervention

This GIS project examines spatial patterns of femicide in Latin America and the Caribbean between 2012 and 2023. Using femicide rates per 100,000 women and absolute data at the city level, a heat map was created to visualize the geographic distribution of these crimes. The aim is to identify areas with high concentrations of gender-based violence and explore correlations with socioeconomic and demographic factors. The findings reveal critical regions where femicide rates are alarmingly high, offering valuable insights for shaping public policies and targeted intervention strategies. This spatial analysis contributes to a deeper understanding of the issue and emphasizes the urgent need for action to combat violence against women in the region.

Ben Simon | Kelp and Oyster Farm Suitability Map of New York Harbor

Ocean farming and habitat restoration is an essential piece to making our coastal cities more resilient to severe storms and rising tides. This report frames the growing of kelp and oysters as farming, though gardening might be a better way to think about it. The purpose of identifying the sites described in this report is not centered on human consumption. Kelp absorbs carbon dioxide from the water, which protects shelled animals from ocean acidification, and they both improve water quality, provide habitat for marine life, and protect coast lines by reducing wave energy. These sites will also be used to educate the public about the connections that link the marine and terrestrial environments. The goal with generating this map is to help expedite site selection and farm expansion.
