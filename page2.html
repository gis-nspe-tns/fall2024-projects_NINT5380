Ingrid Alexandra Nielsen | Amazon rainforest fires and forest loss throughout 2014-2022

The Amazon rainforest faces increasingly severe fire seasons during the dry season, largely driven by deforestation and agricultural expansion. These fires release significant amounts of carbon dioxide, exacerbating climate change and threatening the Amazon's critical role as a major carbon sink. There is growing concern that frequent and intense fires are reducing the forest's ability to absorb greenhouse gases. Analyzing fire patterns over the years can provide insights into the causes and trends of these outbreaks. Therefore, this project aims to analyze the amount of fires and forest loss in Mato Grosso, one of the main agricultural states and third largest one in Brazil, from 2014 to 2022.

Nour Al Safadi | Mapping Crisis in Sudan: Civilians Vulnerability, January 01,2023 to November 14, 2024 spatial analyses

This project examines the ongoing conflict in Sudan after it separated from South Sudan, paying special attention to people living in crowded areas who are most at risk. Covering the period from January 1, 2023, to November 14, 2024, it uses maps, population data, and records of violent events to pinpoint which regions face the greatest danger. By comparing where attacks by the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) take place with where most people live, the project creates a risk map showing which areas are hit hardest. The results show that places with many people—like Khartoum—suffer the most, causing severe humanitarian problems and forcing many people to leave their homes. These findings highlight the urgent need for better humanitarian aid, stronger protections for civilians, and well-informed efforts to prevent harm and demand accountability.

Healani Goo | Tuvalu: Sea Level Rise Vulnerability 2100

Pacific Islands are responsible for less than 1% of total global greenhouse gas emissions yet feel the effects of climate change first and worst. Tuvalu, a small island nation in the Pacific, is among one of the most vulnerable nations to climate change and sea level rise due to its low lying atolls and coastal communities. For this project, I conduct a literature review of possible scenarios of sea level rise and storm surge scenarios and utilize a baseline worst case scenario for global sea level rise in addition to specific projections of sea level rise and storm surge scenarios for the island of Tuvalu to show Tuvalu’s land vulnerability and coastal inundation by and after 2100, in addition to direct population vulnerability.

Tan De Xuan | Where Tsunamis Can't Go

Southeast Asia is especially prone to tsunami hazards because of its geographical location in the 'Pacific Ring of Fire'. The disastrous effect of the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami made explicit and clear the terrible consequences of inadequate warning systems and a lack of preparation for susceptible areas. Here compiled data on elevation models and population distribution within administrative boundaries was used to create a maritime risk map of Southeast Asia that allows indentification of place-based areas of risk.

Aratrika Debnath | Busting the Myths of Climate-Induced Migration

This study examines the dynamics of climate-induced migration through GIS-based mapping to debunk prevailing myths and highlight policy gaps. Contrary to the belief that climate migration mirrors traditional models, findings reveal it is predominantly internal and regional, driven largely by sudden and slow-onset climate events such as floods, droughts, and storms. Disaster-induced displacement surpasses conflict-driven migration, disproportionately affecting vulnerable regions with limited adaptive capacity. Analysis of settlement patterns in India shows migrants favor regional and secondary cities over major urban centers, with many settlements expanding in climate-vulnerable areas. Case studies of Assam's Tezpur and Jorhat post-2022 floods underscore the risks to core urban infrastructure and residential areas developed in recent years. The study highlights the inadequacy of letting go of climate migration as a strong mitigation strategy in the absence of tailored policies for climate migrants. These insights emphasize the urgent need for evidence-based, inclusive frameworks to manage the complexities of climate-induced migration and ensure the safety and stability of affected populations.
